This document was originally prepared as a follow up analysis of the W3C Affiliate Members requested by the Advisory Board during the April 2017 meeting in Beijing.  It provides the data, some analysis of that data and some recommendations on what we might change. It was originally presented to the AB at the F2F on 21 June 2017.

The follow-on was to profile the nine organizations in the Big Organizations category to see what that data might tell us about potential changes to our Membership definitions.

Conclusions:
In looking at this data I think there are a few things to note.  First is that the majority (54%) of Affiliate Members are for-profit organizations that truly fit in that category.  While we could explore tiering that group to move some up in price, getting the actual information we'd need to do that (number of employees, actual revenues, etc.) is challenging as most of them are private corporations so the tools Business Development has to do this research will be minimally helpful.

Next, out of the "big" list there are 5 (BBC, CETC, MITRE, Mozilla, NHK) that we might upgrade if we can find a way to define a rule that makes them ineligible for an Affiliate Membership.  We could put a "campaign" in place to reach out to the senior levels of these organizations to see if they would be interested in being Sponsors.  In the past we've gotten small Sponsorships out of BBC, Mozilla and NHK and I'm not sure of what results an executive-to-executive outreach would have.

The US Federal Reserve is a bit of an edge case as they are not technically part of the US Government but are the central bank of the US.  They have 12 branches and under our current definitions all 12 get to participate in W3C as they are under the single "control" of the Board of Governors.  They do projects that are shared between the Branches but they also see themselves as unique entities.  I think there should be some way to define this type of organization as needing to be a Big Member for W3C.

The other three "big" Members (Cleveland Clinic, Kaiser Permanente, Mayo Clinic) are here primarily for the Semantic Web Health Care and Life Sciences IG who's charter expired in July 2016.  My speculation is that they are paying their $7,900 by default and raising their rates and bringing attention to their Membership could cause them to leave.  While getting one of the other 5 to upgrade to a Full Membership more than offsets this, do we want to have our last fragments of this industry leave?  I'd like to have discussion on this.

I think the bigger opportunity may be with the Association slice which is 30 organizations or 14% of the pool.  While we changed the rules regarding participation for this category of organization we haven't had conversations with them around their participation. We would need to do the research on each of them to see how we move ahead.  My guess is that several have organizations that should be Full or Big Members participating on their behalf.  These organizations are spread across our Hosts and we should have a "campaign" by the Business Development team to reach out to these organizations, explain the rules and see how we collaborate to get the W3C value proposition in front of their Members.  This campaign would only apply to those organizations who's Members are Organizations, not to those organizations who's Members are individuals (i.e. JS Foundation).
